The Sacred Legacy

Naimisharanya the "Forest of Naimisha" is one of the most ancient and sacred forests in India, where the rishis of Vedic times lived, meditated, and composed the foundational texts of Hindu civilization. It is here that the great sage Suta Goswami recited the entire Srimad Bhagavata Purana (18,000 verses) to 88,000 assembled sages an event that established this forest as the supreme centre of sacred knowledge in the Hindu tradition.

The Chakra Teertha a sacred circular pond formed by the rotating discus (Sudarshana Chakra) of Lord Vishnu is the principal sacred site here. The entire 84-kroshi (0 km) circumambulation of this sacred zone is considered one of the most meritorious pilgrimages in Hinduism. The forests, rivers, and sacred ponds of Naimisharanya continue to draw spiritual seekers seeking the meditative peace that ancient sages discovered here.

Chakra Teertha

The principal sacred site a circular kund believed to be formed by Lord Vishnu's Sudarshana Chakra boring into the earth. Its waters are considered extraordinarily purifying. The kund is surrounded by temples and has been the site of continuous worship since Vedic times.

Festivals

The Makar Sankranti fair (January) draws hundreds of thousands. The Kartik Purnima celebration sees the entire Gomati river illuminated with floating lamps, creating one of UP's most beautiful spiritual spectacles.

Vedic Heritage

The Srimad Bhagavata Purana, the Mahabharata recitation, and numerous Upanishads were composed or recited here. The forest is dotted with ancient hermitages (ashrams) and meditation spots associated with legendary sages.

84 Koshi Parikrama

The 84-kroshi (0 km) parikrama of the entire Naimisharanya zone taking 30+ days on foot is considered equivalent in merit to completing all four Char Dhams simultaneously, according to several sacred texts.

In Naimisharanya the trees themselves are sages, the birds carry mantras on their wings, and the very air breathes the accumulated wisdom of ten thousand years of devoted inquiry into the nature of existence. Srimad Bhagavata Purana, 1.1.4
