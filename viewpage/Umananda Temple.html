The Sacred Legacy

The Umananda temple stands on Peacock Island in the middle of the mighty Brahmaputra River claimed to be the world's smallest inhabited river island. Built in 1694 CE by Ahom king Sukhrungphaa (Gadadhar Singha) and restored after an earthquake, the temple is dedicated to Umananda (Shiva delight with Uma) a gentle, loving form of Shiva worshipped with Uma-Parvati at his side. The island is also a protected sanctuary for the rare Golden Langur monkey.

The approach to Umananda by country boat across the wide, powerful Brahmaputra is itself a meditation the broad river, the distant hills of Guwahati, and the emerging view of the temple atop the forested island create a sense of approaching a completely separate sacred world. The island has no vehicles, no commerce, no noise only birdsong, monkey calls, and the sound of temple bells, creating a contrast with Guwahati's busy urban energy that is spiritually electric.

River Island Sanctuary

The Umananda island combines the sacred and the natural in perfect harmony the temple at the summit of the forested hill, the Golden Langurs (considered the rarest primates in the world) moving through the trees, and the Brahmaputra's vast current surrounding all of it. Visiting here is simultaneously a pilgrimage, a nature experience, and a meditation.

Maha Shivaratri

Maha Shivaratri is the temple's principal festival when thousands of devotees make the boat journey across the Brahmaputra to worship Lord Shiva through the night. The island illuminated with temple lamps reflected in the river water creates one of Assam's most beautiful sacred spectacles.

Golden Langurs

The Peacock Island is home to a small population of the Golden Langur one of the world's rarest primates, found only in Assam and Bhutan. These beautiful golden-furred monkeys are considered sacred by devotees and move freely through the temple grounds, sometimes approaching humans without fear.

Nearby Kamakhya

Umananda is traditionally visited in combination with the Kamakhya Devi temple (on Nilachal Hill nearby) the two together representing Shiva and Shakti in the sacred geography of Guwahati. Together they complete the symbolic union of the divine masculine and feminine that is the foundation of Shaiva-Shakta philosophical tradition.

In the middle of the most powerful river in the subcontinent, on the smallest island imaginable Lord Shiva sits with Uma, teaching us that the strength of love is not measured by the size of its container but by the depth of what it holds. Assamese Devotional Verse
