The Sacred Legacy

Gangotri is a prominent Hindu pilgrim-town and one of the Chota Char Dham pilgrimage areas. It is the highest temple dedicated to Goddess Ganga. The serene white marble temple stands as a beacon of spirituality amidst the majestic Garhwal Himalayas.

The current temple structure was built by Adi Shankaracharya in the 8th century CE. The Pandavas are said to have sought Lord Shiva's forgiveness here after the Kurukshetra war. Lord Shiva, in the form of a bull, dived into the earth here different body parts emerging at the five Panch Kedar shrines across the Garhwal Himalayas.

Sacred Architecture

Built of enormous perfectly fitted grey stone slabs an extraordinary engineering achievement at such altitude. The rectangular temple with conical Shikhara has survived centuries of Himalayan winters and the catastrophic 2013 flood that destroyed the surrounding town but left the temple miraculously intact a fact that strengthened the faith of millions across India.

Festivals & Celebrations

The temple opens on Akshaya Tritiya (April-May) with elaborate ceremonies. Maha Shivaratri is celebrated with great devotion. The doors close for winter on Bhaishnav Chaturdashi, when a divine lamp is lit and burns continuously through the six winter months when the temple is under deep snow and inaccessible.

Origin of the Ganges

The temple represents the origin of the holiest river in Hinduism, the Ganges. The actual source, Gaumukh, is situated in the Gangotri glacier. The temple's pure white structure symbolizes the purity of Goddess Ganga herself.

The Sacred Trek

The 16-km trek from Gaurikund through the Kedar Valley is itself a spiritual purification. Pilgrims walk through forests, mountain streams, and alpine meadows. Pony and palanquin services are available. A helicopter service from Phata, Guptkashi, or Sitapur makes the journey accessible for devotees unable to trek the demanding route.

Bathing in the holy waters of the Ganges here washes away the sins of a lifetime. Ancient Scriptures
