The Sacred Legacy

The Lotus Temple in New Delhi is one of the most visited and architecturally celebrated buildings in the world a Bahai House of Worship that has won numerous architectural awards and welcomed over 100 million visitors since its completion in 1986. Designed by Iranian architect Fariborz Sahba, the structure takes the form of an unfolding lotus flower a symbol of purity and divinity across multiple world religions.

The temple consists of 27 free-standing marble-clad petals arranged in clusters of three to form nine sides. Nine reflecting pools surround the structure, giving the appearance of the lotus floating on water. No idol, image, or religious symbol is placed within only scriptural readings and silent prayer are permitted, making it a truly universal sacred space where all of humanity can converge.

Architectural Marvel

The structure uses 800 tonnes of hand-selected white Pentelic marble sourced from the same quarry as the Parthenon in Greece. The 27 petals rise 34.27 metres, enclosed by nine pools. The interior central hall seats 2,500 people under a soaring 34-metre dome with no internal supports a feat of pure structural engineering.

Universal Significance

The Bahai faith teaches the unity of all religions and the oneness of humanity. The Lotus Temple embodies this teaching physically it has no restrictions on who may enter, pray, or meditate within. Christians, Hindus, Muslims, Sikhs, Jews, and people of no specific faith have all found peace within its serene white marble walls.

Awards & Recognition

The Lotus Temple has won the Craftsmanship Award from the Institute of Structural Engineers (UK), the Paul Waterbury Award for Architectural Excellence, and was featured in virtually every major architectural magazine worldwide. It was awarded the title of "Most Visited Building in the World" in multiple years.

Spiritual Practice

Visitors are invited to enter in silence and spend time in quiet prayer or meditation. Short scriptural readings from the Bahai writings are shared at specified times. Photography is permitted outside but not within the inner hall, preserving the deeply contemplative atmosphere that makes this space uniquely transformative.
