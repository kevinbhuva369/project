The Sacred Legacy

The Lakshmi Narayan Temple popularly known as Birla Mandir is one of Delhi's most magnificent and beloved temples, inaugurated on April 7, 1939 by Mahatma Gandhi himself, who made it a condition of his attendance that the temple be open to people of all castes and communities. Built by the prominent industrialist Jugal Kishore Birla, this gleaming white and red sandstone complex stands as a landmark of modern Delhi's spiritual and architectural heritage.

The temple is dedicated to Lord Vishnu (Narayan) and Goddess Lakshmi, and features the Lord in the centre flanked by Shri Durgaji on one side and Shri Shiv-Parvati on the other. The complex is spread over 7.5 acres and features beautiful gardens, fountains, and multiple shrines dedicated to various deities. Mahatma Gandhi's inauguration of this temple at the height of India's independence movement made it a symbol of religious democracy and social equality.

Sacred Architecture

The temple blends the Nagara architectural style (North Indian) with Orissa temple influences, primarily using white Makrana marble with red and ochre sandstone accents. The main Shikhara rises 47 metres. The walls feature narrative panels depicting scenes from the Mahabharata, Ramayana, Bhagavata Purana, and Gita Saar making the temple itself a complete visual scripture for the devotee.

Gandhi's Inauguration

Mahatma Gandhi's condition for inaugurating the temple that it be open to all castes and communities without discrimination was revolutionary for 1939 India. This act transformed the temple into a spiritual democracy, and the tradition of welcoming all visitors regardless of faith, caste, or background has been maintained without exception for over 85 years making it one of Delhi's most inclusive sacred spaces.

Festivals & Celebrations

Diwali at Birla Mandir is spectacular thousands of earthen lamps illuminate the entire complex and surrounding gardens, creating a magical atmosphere visible from Connaught Place. Janmashtami, Navratri, and Ram Navami are celebrated with elaborate rituals and large public gatherings. The temple's central Delhi location makes all its festivals easily accessible to the city's vast population.

The Temple Gardens

The 7.5-acre garden complex surrounding the temple features fountains, geometric flower beds, and peaceful meditation areas. The garden is particularly beautiful during the spring flower season. Small shrines to Hanuman, Ganesha, Buddha, and Mahavira are distributed across the grounds an expression of the Birla family's commitment to spiritual harmony and unity across India's diverse religious traditions.

A temple that closes its doors to any man closes its doors to God. The divine dwells in every human heart without distinction a temple that recognises this truth is a true house of God. Mahatma Gandhi, at the inauguration, 1939
