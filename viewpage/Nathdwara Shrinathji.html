The Sacred Legacy

Nathdwara "Gateway of the Lord" is home to the most beloved form of Lord Krishna: Shrinathji, depicted as a 7-year-old boy lifting the Govardhan mountain to protect the Braj people from Indra's wrath. The black stone idol, believed to be self-manifested (Swayambhu) and thousands of years old, was brought to Nathdwara from Mathura in 1691 CE by Vallabhacharya's descendants to protect it from Mughal persecution.

According to divine legend, the chariot carrying the idol got stuck in the mud at the spot that would become Nathdwara which was interpreted as a divine sign that this was where the Lord wished to reside. The temple and the surrounding town grew organically around this divine choice. Today, Nathdwara is one of the wealthiest and most visited temples in Rajasthan, drawing hundreds of thousands of Pushti Marg Vaishnavas annually.

Pichwai Paintings

The extraordinary Pichwai (backdrop paintings) tradition of Nathdwara is one of India's greatest living art forms large, intricately detailed paintings of Shrinathji that change with the seasons and festivals, placed behind the idol during Darshan. Nathdwara's Pichwai artists are considered masters of a tradition spanning 300+ years, with paintings that can take up to a year to complete.

Eight Daily Darshans

The Pushti Marg tradition prescribes eight specific Darshan times each day each presenting Shrinathji in a different divine mood and context: Mangala (awakening), Shringar (adornment), Gwal (return from grazing), Raj Bhoga (royal feast), Utthapana (afternoon rest end), Bhog (evening offering), Sandhya (dusk), and Shayana (bedtime). Each Darshan tells a chapter of the divine child's daily life.

Pushti Marg Philosophy

Founded by Shri Vallabhacharya in the 16th century, the Pushti Marg (path of grace) philosophy teaches that the highest form of devotion is treating the Lord as a beloved child nurturing, adorning, feeding, and playing with the divine. This "seva" (loving service) tradition expressed at Nathdwara is the most refined form of this philosophy, practiced with extraordinary artistry and devotion.

Sacred Prasad & Culture

The Panchamrut and various food items offered to Shrinathji are believed to carry extraordinary divine blessings. The town of Nathdwara itself is entirely devoted to the Lord every shop, institution, and activity revolves around the temple's schedule. Nathdwara is also famous for its silver work, marble carvings, and the distinctive Rajasthani miniature paintings it has inspired for centuries.

He who lifts mountains as a child's game, he who dances in the rain with his beloveds, he whose mere glance dissolves the boundaries of time this is Shrinathji, the lord of pure grace. Vallabhacharya, Sanskrit Stotra
