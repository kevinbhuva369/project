Foundation

What is a Hindu Temple?

Understanding the concept of a Mandir goes far beyond religion — it is architecture, philosophy, science, and art in perfect unity.

A Hindu temple is far more than a building. Known as Mandir, Devasthana, or Devalaya (the abode of God), it is a living cosmological symbol — a physical model of the entire universe constructed according to precise sacred mathematical formulas documented in the ancient Vastushastra and Agama Shastras.

When a devotee enters a temple, they symbolically re-enter the cosmos itself — progressing from the outer world (ordinary consciousness) through successive enclosures into the innermost sanctum (Garbhagriha — literally "womb-chamber") where the divine resides in pure, concentrated form.

Historically, temples were not only sacred spaces. They functioned as centres of education, art, music, dance, economy, governance, and community life — the complete beating heart of Indian civilization for 2,000+ years.

Cosmic Map in Stone

The temple's floor plan is a mandala — a diagram of the entire cosmos. The central Garbhagriha represents the cosmic womb where creation begins. Moving outward, each successive hall represents a lower dimension of consciousness — the physical journey through the temple is a spiritual journey inward.

Vastu Shastra

Every temple is precisely oriented — usually facing east to receive the sun's first rays on the divine idol. The mathematical ratios of width, height, and depth are prescribed down to finger-widths in the Vastu Shastra. This creates structures that exist in perfect mathematical harmony with natural forces.

Community Centre

Ancient temples ran schools (Gurukuls), hospitals, granaries, musical conservatories, dance academies, and courts of justice within their precincts. Temples collected taxes, managed irrigation systems, and were the economic engines of their communities — a complete civilization in miniature.

Sacred Design

The Architecture of the Divine

Every element of temple architecture encodes a precise spiritual and scientific meaning. Nothing is decorative — everything is intentional.

🗼

Shikhara / Gopuram (Tower)

The temple tower represents Mount Meru — the cosmic mountain at the centre of the universe in Hindu cosmology. Its upward thrust toward the sky represents the human aspiration toward the divine. In the North Indian style, the Shikhara curves inward (Nagara). In the South, the Gopuram rises as a stepped pyramid (Dravidian).

🚪

Gopuram Gateway

The ornate gateway towers of South Indian temples are covered in thousands of painted sculptures of gods, demons, saints, and cosmic scenes — representing the entire range of existence that the devotee must traverse before reaching the divine centre. Entering the temple through a Gopuram is a symbolic birth into the sacred world.

🕳️

Garbhagriha (Inner Sanctum)

The smallest, darkest room in the temple — the inner sanctuary — is the most sacred. The deliberate dimness and lack of decoration focuses the mind completely on the divine idol within. The term "Garbhagriha" (womb-chamber) indicates this is the source from which the divine energy of the entire temple radiates outward.

Mandapa (Hall)

The columned halls (Mandapas) that precede the inner sanctum are spaces for congregation, music, dance, and discourse. The Natamandira (dance hall) was where classical dance — itself a sacred offering — was performed before the deity. The carved pillars of ancient Mandapas are encyclopedias of mythology, ethics, and divine beauty.

Temple Tank (Pushkarini)

The sacred water tank adjacent to virtually every South Indian temple serves for ritual purification before entering the temple. The tank's waters are consecrated with mantras and the collected blessings of centuries of worship. Bathing here is considered equivalent to bathing in all sacred rivers simultaneously.

🔔

The Temple Bell (Ghanta)

The large bronze or brass bell hung at the temple entrance is struck by devotees as they enter. Scientifically, the specific alloy and shape produces a sound that resonates at 7 seconds — proven to simultaneously activate both hemispheres of the brain and induce a meditative theta-wave state. This is not coincidence — it is ancient neuro-acoustic engineering.

The Sacred Science of the Kalasha

The Kalasha — the pot-shaped finial at the very top of the Shikhara — is not merely decorative. It is made of a specific copper-brass alloy carefully described in the Agama Shastra, and contains consecrated water, specific herbs, and inscribed copper plates. Modern electromagnetic studies have found that the Kalasha acts as a resonant antenna for specific frequencies, concentrating and amplifying the devotional energy generated within the temple complex.

Living Tradition

Sacred Rituals — The Daily Divine Schedule

Hindu temple worship follows a precise daily timetable called the Puja schedule — treating the deity as a living royal being who wakes, eats, rests, and sleeps.

Mangala Aarti (Dawn)

The first puja of the day — conducted before sunrise — wakens the deity. The inner sanctum is opened, lamps lit, and gentle wake-up songs sung. Only the most devoted pilgrims attend this early puja, which carries the freshest, most powerful spiritual charge of the day.

Abhishekam (Sacred Bath)

The sacred bathing of the deity with Panchamrut (five nectars — milk, curd, honey, ghee, and sugar) followed by water is the most intimate ritual act in temple worship. The five substances correspond to the five elements and five senses — the offering represents the complete surrender of one's sensory existence.

Shringar (Adornment)

The deity is dressed in fresh silk garments, adorned with gold and gemstone jewellery, garlanded with specific flowers prescribed for each deity, anointed with sandalwood paste, and presented as a complete divine being in royal splendour for the day's darshan.

Bhog / Naivedya (Food Offering)

Up to 56 varieties of food (Chappan Bhog) are offered to the deity at the midday meal — cooked in the temple kitchen using sacred recipes, offered with specific mantras, and then distributed as Prasad to waiting devotees. The food is believed to carry the divine's direct blessing after the offering.

Aarti (Light Offering)

The Aarti ceremony — waving a multi-flame lamp in a specific clockwise circular motion before the deity — is accompanied by bell-ringing, conch-blowing, and devotional singing. The circling lamp symbolises offering the entire solar system to the divine. The combined sound is scientifically shown to create a measurable energy shift in the space.

Pradakshina (Circumambulation)

Walking clockwise around the sanctum keeps the divine always on your right — the side of reverence in Indian tradition. Each circumambulation is a meditation in motion. The number of circles prescribed (3, 7, 11, 21, or 108 depending on the deity and occasion) each carry specific symbolic significance.

Pushpa Puja (Flower Offering)

Each flower offered has specific significance: Lotus for Lakshmi and Vishnu (purity from mud), Bilva leaves for Shiva (the three leaflets represent the three Gunas surrendered), Marigold for Durga (the brilliant orange represents the fire of devotion), Jasmine for Krishna (fragrance representing divine love spreading in all directions).

Shayana Seva (Night Rest)

The final puja of the day lovingly prepares the deity for sleep — the inner sanctum is dimmed, a final lamp waved, lullaby-like devotional songs sung, and the deity's curtain gently drawn closed. This tender anthropomorphic relationship between devotee and divine is the living heart of the Bhakti tradition.

Regional Heritage

The Six Grand Styles of Temple Architecture

India's astonishing regional diversity produced at least six distinct temple architectural traditions — each a complete aesthetic universe in itself.

Nagara (North India)

Curvilinear Shikhara resembling mountain peaks. Examples: Khajuraho, Lingaraja, Kedarnath, Somnath.

Dravidian (South India)

Tall pyramidal Gopurams covered in colourful sculptures. Examples: Meenakshi, Srirangam, Brihadeeswara.

Vesara (Deccan)

Hybrid style blending North and South elements. Examples: Belur, Halebid, Pattadakal (Chalukya-Hoysala).

Kerala Style

Sloped copper roofs adapted for heavy monsoon. Examples: Guruvayur, Padmanabhaswamy, Aranmula.

Rock-Cut Temples

Entire temples carved from single living rock. Examples: Ellora Kailasa, Shore Temple, Elephanta Caves.

Modern Marble Style

Contemporary with Italian marble, traditional form. Examples: Akshardham Delhi, Prem Mandir, Birla Mandirs.

Complete Guide

The Sacred Pilgrimage — Step by Step

Tirtha Yatra (pilgrimage) is not merely travel — it is a complete inner journey. Here is how to do it with full understanding and maximum spiritual benefit.

01

Sankalpa — The Sacred Resolve

Before departure, make a formal mental declaration (Sankalpa) of your pilgrimage purpose — whether healing, gratitude, guidance, or liberation. A clearly held intention amplifies every experience that follows. The Sankalpa is the spiritual ignition of the pilgrimage.

02

Purification — Body and Mind

Observe at least three days before the pilgrimage of vegetarian eating, early rising, reduced speech, and if possible, a partial fast. This is not punishment — it is tuning the instrument of your body and mind to the higher frequencies of the sacred site you are about to enter.

03

Sacred Snan — The Ritual Bath

At every sacred river, tank, or kund on the route — take a physical bath with mantras. Water is the great purifier physically and subtly. Ancient tradition prescribes this specifically because sacred bodies of water near major temples have measurably different mineral and energetic composition than ordinary water.

04

Darshan — The Sacred Exchange

Stand before the deity with eyes open, inner chatter silenced, and full presence offered. Darshan (divine sight) is an exchange — the devotee sees the divine, and the divine sees the devotee. This is not metaphorical — it is the precise moment of sacred contact that the entire pilgrimage has been building toward.

05

Pradakshina — Circumambulation

Walk clockwise around the sanctum — 3, 7, 11, or 21 times depending on the tradition. Keep the divine always on your right. Each circle integrates the experience more deeply and builds the accumulated sacred energy of the visit. The body's movement encodes the experience in muscle memory and cellular consciousness.

06

Prasad — The Divine Gift

Receive the sacred Prasad (food blessed by the divine's presence) and consume it there or shortly after. Prasad is not merely symbolic — it is the divine energy in physical form, transferred through the ritual of offering and returned through the ritual of sharing. This completes the closed circuit of sacred exchange.

5,000 Years in Brief

The Grand Timeline of Temple Tradition

From Vedic fire altars to Akshardham — the 5,000-year unbroken story of India's sacred architectural journey.

3000 BCE

Indus Valley Sacred Structures

The Great Bath at Mohenjo-daro — possibly India's first sacred water structure — shows that ritual purification and sacred space were central concerns of the subcontinent's earliest civilization. Fire altars at Kalibangan suggest organised Vedic-style ritual from the third millennium BCE.

1500 BCE

Vedic Fire Altars (Yajnas)

The Rigvedic tradition worshipped in open-air fire altars (Yajnas) without permanent structures. The Agni Kunda (fire altar) itself was the mobile, temporary temple — built from bricks arranged in precise geometric patterns (Sulbasutras) that represent the earliest known systematic geometry in human history.

300 BCE

First Structural Temples

The Mauryan period (300 BCE) sees the transition from temporary sacred structures to permanent stone temples. The earliest known stone temples in India date to this period — marking the beginning of the 2,300-year tradition of stone temple construction that continues to the present day.

300–700 CE

Gupta Golden Age

The Gupta Empire (320–550 CE) is India's temple architecture golden age — the Dashavatara Temple at Deogarh, the Udayagiri caves, and the Bhitargaon brick temple represent the emergence of the distinctive Indian temple aesthetic. The Agama Shastras (temple science manuals) are codified in this period.

700–1100 CE

Pallava, Chalukya & Chola Mastery

The Shore Temple (700 CE), Kailasanatha Kanchipuram (720 CE), the Pattadakal complex (740 CE), and the Brihadeeswara Thanjavur (1010 CE) represent the absolute pinnacle of Indian temple architecture — engineering achievements that remain astonishing to contemporary architects and engineers.

900–1300 CE

Khajuraho, Konark & North Indian Peak

The Chandela dynasty's Khajuraho temples (950–1050 CE) and the Ganga dynasty's Sun Temple Konark (1250 CE) represent North Indian temple architecture at its most exuberant — every inch carved with mythological, devotional, erotic, and cosmic imagery of extraordinary technical virtuosity.

1336–1565 CE

Vijayanagara Empire — The Great Protector

The Vijayanagara Empire (capital: Hampi) was the last great Hindu empire — its rulers rebuilt and expanded hundreds of temples destroyed during earlier invasions. The Virupaksha Temple, Vitthala Temple, and Lepakshi Temple represent this era's extraordinary combination of devotional intensity and artistic sophistication.

1600–1800 CE

Maratha Revival & Reconstruction

The Maratha Empire under Shivaji and the Peshwas, and figures like Ahilyabai Holkar, undertook mass reconstruction of temples destroyed during the Mughal period. Kashi Vishwanath, Grishneshwar, Somnath, and scores of important temples were rebuilt or substantially renovated in this era of Hindu cultural renaissance.

1900–Present

Modern Temple Renaissance

The 20th and 21st centuries see a new golden age of temple construction — Birla Mandirs across India (1930s onward), ISKCON Bangalore (1997), Akshardham Gandhinagar (1992), Akshardham Delhi (2005), and the magnificent Ram Mandir Ayodhya (2024) demonstrate that India's sacred building tradition is not merely surviving but vibrantly alive.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

The most common questions visitors have about Hindu temples — answered simply and clearly.

Why do we remove shoes before entering a temple?
Shoes carry the dust and energy of the outside world — the marketplace, the street, the ordinary. Removing them at the threshold is a physical act of transition: leaving the world of commerce and distraction and entering the world of the sacred. The temple floor itself is considered consecrated ground — sanctified by decades or centuries of worship energy that has soaked into the very stone. It is also practically hygienic — keeping the altar area clean.
Why do we ring the bell when entering a temple?
The bell's 7-second resonance (enabled by a specific 5-metal alloy called "bell metal") simultaneously activates both brain hemispheres, inducing a meditative theta-wave state. This was ancient neuro-acoustic engineering, not mere ceremony. The bell sound also announces your arrival to the deity, disperses the mental chatter of the journey, and creates a sonic boundary between the outer and inner worlds. In large temples, the combined sound of multiple massive bells ringing together creates a measurable shift in acoustic energy throughout the complex.
What is Darshan and why is it so important?
Darshan (literally "sight") is the sacred act of beholding the divine image — and simultaneously being seen by the divine. In Hindu theology, the eyes exchange energy — the blessed sight of the divine enters the devotee, and the wholehearted gaze of the devotee enters the divine. This is a genuine two-directional sacred exchange, not a metaphor. The devotee approaches, the curtain opens, and for a moment the two worlds make contact. This is the entire purpose of the pilgrimage — that one moment of direct sacred encounter.
What is Pradakshina (circumambulation) and what does it mean?
Pradakshina is the act of walking clockwise around the deity's sanctum, keeping the divine always on your right — the side of respect in Indian tradition. Each circle has several levels of meaning: physically, it keeps you moving in the direction the sun moves, aligning you with natural cycles; spiritually, it means "the divine is the centre of my existence — I revolve around the divine as planets revolve around the sun." The number of circles (3, 7, 11, 21, 108) varies by deity and occasion — each number carrying specific sacred significance.
What is Prasad and why is it considered sacred?
Prasad (literally "grace" or "gracious gift") is food first offered to the deity through specific mantras and ritual, and then distributed to devotees as the divine's own "remnant." The principle is that whatever the divine's presence touches takes on the divine's quality. Prasad therefore carries the specific energy of the deity's presence in concentrated, edible form. The famous Tirupati Laddu, Puri Jagannath's Mahaprasad, and Nathdwara's Panjiri are considered specially potent examples — sought by millions and believed to carry healing, blessing, and transformative power in themselves.
Can non-Hindus visit Hindu temples?
The vast majority of Hindu temples throughout India are open to respectful visitors of all faiths and nationalities — the emphasis is placed on correct attitude (reverent, dress code observed, no disruption of ritual) rather than religious identity. However, a few notable exceptions exist: the Jagannath Temple in Puri and certain South Indian temples (Srirangam, Madurai Meenakshi's inner sanctum) have traditions restricting entry to non-Hindus in the innermost areas. These are older, tradition-specific policies. If in doubt, observe the posted notices or ask at the temple entrance.
What is the correct dress code for visiting a temple?
General guidelines: Shoulders and knees should be covered (both men and women). Traditional Indian dress (saree, salwar kameez, kurta-dhoti) is always appropriate. Many South Indian temples specifically require men to remove their shirts and women to wear a saree or other traditional attire before entering the inner sanctum — dhoti or mundu is provided free at temple entrances. Avoid leather items (some temples prohibit leather belts and bags inside due to ahimsa principles). Cleanliness of clothing is important — fresh or at minimum clean garments are preferred.
What is the significance of the Shivlinga?
"Linga" in Sanskrit means "symbol" or "mark" — not the anatomical interpretation sometimes suggested. The Shivlinga represents Shiva's formless, infinite, transcendent aspect — consciousness that has no beginning or end, no shape or gender, no form or feature. According to the Linga Purana, the universe itself emerged from the cosmic Linga of fire (Jyotirlinga). The cylindrical form mounted on the circular Yoni (base/source) represents the eternal union of consciousness (Shiva) and creative energy (Shakti) — the polarity from which all existence continuously emerges and into which it continuously returns.
Why are temples built facing east?
Most Hindu temples are oriented east-facing (toward sunrise) for multiple reasons simultaneously: astronomically, the first rays of the rising sun fall directly on the divine idol in the inner sanctum — the daily "divine awakening" by solar light; philosophically, east is the direction from which light and knowledge arise (east = "purva" — the first direction); practically, the orientation ensures the temple structure casts its shadow west, keeping the circumambulation path on the cooler, shaded side for much of the day. Some temples — like Mahakaleshwar Ujjain — face south for specific theological reasons related to Shiva's cosmic role.
Why are temples always built near rivers or water bodies?
Rivers represent the continuity of life, the flow of time, and the purifying power of moving water. Practically, flowing water was essential for the daily Abhishekam (sacred bathing of the deity), for ritual cleansing of devotees, and for the massive food preparations of temple kitchens serving thousands daily. Energetically, ancient Vastu Shastra holds that flowing water adjacent to a temple amplifies and distributes the temple's energy field throughout the surrounding landscape. Many major temples — Kashi Vishwanath (Ganga), Tirupati (Swarnamukhi River), Srirangam (Kaveri) — are literally built within a meander curve, creating a natural sacred island.
What is the difference between a Mandir, Devasthana, Kovil, and Derasar?
"Mandir" is the North Indian (Hindi/Sanskrit) term, from "mandira" meaning a dwelling or hall. "Devasthana" (Sanskrit: deva = divine, sthana = place/abode) is the classical Sanskrit term found in ancient texts and official temple inscriptions. "Kovil" or "Koyil" is the Tamil word, from "Ko" (King/God) + "il" (house) — the King's dwelling. "Derasar" is the Jain term for their temple. All refer to the same category of sacred architectural space designed to make the divine accessible in physical form — reflecting India's extraordinary linguistic diversity within a unified sacred culture.
What is Abhishekam and why is it performed?
Abhishekam (sacred bathing) is the ritual pouring of specific substances over the divine idol: Panchamrut (milk, curd, honey, ghee, sugar), followed by water, coconut water, sandalwood water, and rose water. Each substance corresponds to one of the five elements and one of the five senses — so the offering is complete surrender of the entire sensory existence. Beyond symbolism, the specific substances used have demonstrated antimicrobial properties — keeping stone idols hygienically clean while also providing a regular infusion of organic compounds that the stone slowly absorbs over centuries, darkening and enriching it with a distinctive patina of devotion.
What is the significance of the 108 number in Hindu tradition?
108 is encoded throughout Hindu sacred mathematics: 108 Upanishads (philosophical texts), 108 beads on a mala (prayer rosary), 108 divine names in most Ashottoram (108-name hymns), 108 sacred Vaishnava shrines (Divya Desams), 108 forms of Shiva's dance, 108 sacred acupressure points in the body. The cosmic connection: Earth-to-Sun distance = 108 × Sun's diameter; Earth-to-Moon distance = 108 × Moon's diameter. This makes 108 literally a cosmic constant connecting Earth, Sun, and Moon in a single mathematical ratio — what ancient seers encoded in every rosary, every ritual count, every sacred architecture proportion.
What is a Shakti Peetha and why are there 51 of them?
Shakti Peethas are sacred sites where body parts of Goddess Sati are believed to have fallen when Lord Shiva carried her grief-stricken corpse through the cosmos and Lord Vishnu sent his Sudarshana Chakra to dismember it (to release Shiva from his paralysing grief). Each fallen body part consecrated the landscape where it landed — creating a sacred geography of 51 (some traditions say 108) powerful Devi sites spread across the entire Indian subcontinent and neighbouring countries. Together the 51 Shakti Peethas form a sacred mandala covering the entire subcontinent with the goddess's body — making the entire land itself a living temple.
What is the difference between Shaiva, Vaishnava, and Shakta temples?
Shaiva temples (dedicated to Lord Shiva) feature the Shivlinga as the primary icon, often with Nandi (the bull) in the entrance corridor, Bilva leaves as primary offerings, and Abhishekam as the primary ritual. Vaishnava temples (dedicated to Lord Vishnu and his avatars) feature standing or reclining Vishnu icons, Tulasi (basil) as primary offering, and emphasise Stotra (hymn) chanting. Shakta temples (dedicated to the Goddess in her various forms) feature fierce or gentle Devi icons, red flowers and kumkum as primary offerings, and particularly intense worship during Navratri. Many temples are blend-types — worshipping multiple deities in separate shrines.
What is a Char Dham pilgrimage and who should do it?
Char Dham ("four abodes of the divine") refers to India's four most sacred pilgrimage sites — Badrinath (Vishnu, North — Uttarakhand), Dwarka (Krishna, West — Gujarat), Puri (Jagannath, East — Odisha), and Rameswaram (Shiva, South — Tamil Nadu). Together they represent the four cardinal directions and four aspects of the divine. The traditional belief is that completing the Char Dham pilgrimage — ideally clockwise from east (Puri) — purifies all accumulated karma and brings liberation (moksha) at death. It is traditionally undertaken at least once in a lifetime, ideally in old age when family responsibilities are complete.
What are the 12 Jyotirlingas and why are they special?
Jyotirlingas (literally "Columns of Light") are 12 sacred sites where Lord Shiva is believed to have manifested as an infinite pillar of fire — transcending the universe from below the Earth to above the sky, visible to both Brahma and Vishnu simultaneously. This mythological event established Shiva as the supreme, boundless cosmic principle. Today, the 12 Jyotirlinga temples — from Somnath (Gujarat) to Ramanathaswamy (Tamil Nadu) — are considered the most potent Shiva shrines in existence. Visiting all 12 is said to purify all sins of all lifetimes simultaneously and guarantee liberation.
What is the best time to visit major temples?
Generally: October to March (winter) is ideal for most temples in terms of weather and crowd management. Avoid peak festivals unless experiencing them is the specific purpose of your visit — the same temples can have 10x their normal crowds during Navratri, Maha Shivaratri, or specific car festivals. Early morning (first 2 hours after opening) gives the most peaceful, least crowded darshan and the freshest, most energetically charged atmosphere. For specific festivals: Maha Shivaratri for Shiva temples, Ram Navami for Rama temples, Janmashtami for Krishna temples, and any Navratri for Shakti temples.
Why do temples have so many deities — is Hinduism polytheistic?
Hindu philosophy is technically Henotheistic (or Monistic Monotheism with polytheistic expressions) — the foundational philosophical position is that there is one infinite, formless Reality (Brahman) that manifests in infinite forms. Each deity — Shiva, Vishnu, Durga, Ganesha, Saraswati — is a specific quality or aspect of this single infinite Reality made accessible to human devotion. The multiplicity of deities is not a contradiction of the One — it is the expression of the One's infinite richness. The great 20th century saint Ramakrishna demonstrated this by achieving liberation through Kali, Rama, and even Christianity — different paths leading to the same summit.
What is the meaning of the Om symbol carved on temples?
The symbol OM (also written AUM) represents the three fundamental states of consciousness — A (waking), U (dream), M (deep sleep) — unified in the silence that follows their utterance (Turiya — the fourth state of pure awareness that underlies all three). It is also the phonetic representation of the sound from which the universe was created (the first vibration of creation), and the sound of the universe's own electromagnetic frequency. OM is placed on temple entrances as a reminder that entering the temple is entering the space of pure consciousness — the state from which all creation emerges and into which all creation returns.
How should I prepare before going on a pilgrimage?
Traditional preparation (Tirtha Yatra preparation): 1) Make a formal Sankalpa — a clear mental declaration of your pilgrimage purpose, 2) Observe purity for 3 days before departure (vegetarian food, early rising, reduced entertainment), 3) Read or listen to stories about the deity of the temple you're visiting (builds devotional context and emotional resonance), 4) Travel light — pilgrimage is about shedding, not accumulating, 5) Approach with a specific prayer or intention rather than going "generally" — the tradition holds that the divine grants what is sincerely asked with a clear heart, not what is vaguely wished for from a distracted mind.
Why do people donate to temples — where does the money go?
Temple donations (Dakshina) in India fund an extraordinary range of public services operated by temple trusts: free food (Annadanam) for pilgrims and the poor (Jagannath Puri feeds 10,000 people free every single day), free hospitals and clinics, schools and colleges, orphanages, water conservation projects, and cultural institutions. The Tirumala Tirupati Devasthanams (TTD) — India's richest temple — runs 65 educational institutions, hundreds of free hospitals, thousands of scholarships, and provides free meals to 50,000+ daily. Contrary to common perception, India's major temple trusts are among the most efficient large-scale social welfare institutions in the country.
